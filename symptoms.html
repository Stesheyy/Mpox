Symptoms of Mpox

Understanding the symptoms of Mpox is essential for early detection, treatment, and preventing its spread.

What is Mpox?

Mpox is a viral infection characterized by a distinct rash and flu-like symptoms. It primarily spreads through close contact and can affect various parts of the body. Early identification of symptoms can aid in timely medical intervention.

Early Symptoms

The early stages of Mpox often resemble flu-like symptoms and can appear within 5-21 days after exposure. These may include:

  • Fever
  • Headache
  • Muscle aches and backache
  • Chills and sweats
  • Fatigue and lack of energy
  • Swollen lymph nodes (a distinguishing feature)

Rash Development

One of the defining characteristics of Mpox is a rash that typically appears a few days after the onset of fever and other symptoms. The rash goes through several stages:

  • Macules: Flat, red spots on the skin
  • Papules: Raised bumps, often itchy or uncomfortable
  • Vesicles: Fluid-filled blisters
  • Pustules: Blisters that become pus-filled and may cause pain
  • Scabs: Pustules dry out and form scabs before eventually healing

The rash often starts on the face before spreading to other parts of the body, including the palms of hands and soles of feet.

Severe Symptoms

While many cases of Mpox are mild, some individuals may experience more severe symptoms. Vulnerable groups such as young children, pregnant women, and individuals with compromised immune systems are at higher risk of complications, which may include:

  • Skin infections from open sores
  • Respiratory distress or cough
  • Secondary bacterial infections
  • In rare cases, complications affecting other organs

Symptom Timeline

The progression of Mpox symptoms typically follows a set timeline:

  • Incubation Period: 5-21 days with no symptoms
  • Prodromal Stage: 1-4 days of flu-like symptoms, swollen lymph nodes
  • Rash Stage: 2-4 weeks of rash development and healing

When to Seek Medical Attention

If you experience any of these symptoms, especially a rash with fever, swollen lymph nodes, or exposure to an infected individual, it is important to consult a healthcare professional promptly. Early diagnosis and treatment can reduce the risk of complications and prevent transmission.
